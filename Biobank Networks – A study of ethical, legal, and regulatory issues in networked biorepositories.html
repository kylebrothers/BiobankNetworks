Biobank Networks

Networked biorepositories make it possible for researchers to access large, diverse collections of biospecimens and health data across multiple institutions. These networks also raise complex issues related to consent and donor permission, privacy and data security, and data access. In this project, entitled “Addressing Ethical Challenges in Biorepository Networks,” we are developing a detailed account of these ethical, legal, and regulatory challenges and identifying solutions to address them. We hope this work will enable biobank networks, both new and old, to meet the needs of donors while at the same time pursuing important health research.

MEET OUR TEAM

Our project is a collaboration of investigators from Case Western Reserve University, University of Louisville, University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, and the Mayo Clinic.

KYLE BROTHERSCo-Principal Investigator
Kyle Brothers, MD, PhD is an Assistant Professor of Pediatrics at the University of Louisville and an affiliated faculty member at the Institute for Bioethics, Health Policy, and Law. Dr. Brothers received his MD from the University of Louisville School of Medicine. He completed his residency training and chief residency in Pediatrics at Vanderbilt Children’s Hospital, and his PhD in Ethics and Society at Vanderbilt University.

Dr. Brothers’ research focuses on policy and ethics in human genetics and the translation of health technologies into clinical care. He is currently the lead investigator for the ethics component of “Genomic Diagnosis in Children with Intellectual Disability,” one of the projects comprising the Clinical Sequencing Exploratory Research (CSER) Consortium. In this project he is examining the implications of sequencing technologies for families seeking a genetic diagnosis for their child’s developmental disability. Dr. Brothers is a practicing primary care pediatrician, and serves as the chair of the ethics committee at Norton Children’s Hospital in Louisville, Kentucky.

AARON GOLDENBERGCo-Principal Investigator
Dr. Aaron Goldenberg is the Director of Research and Associate Professor of Bioethics at the Department of Bioethics at Case Western Reserve University School of Medicine. He is also the Associate Director of the Center for Genetic Research Ethics and Law. Dr. Goldenberg leads the Ethics Core for the Institute for Computational Biology and is an Associate Professor in the Department of Bioethics at Case Western Reserve University School of Medicine. He earned his PhD in Bioethics at Case Western Reserve University. Since joining the faculty at Case Western Reserve University, Dr. Goldenberg’s work has focused on the ethical, legal, and social implications of genetics and genomics in clinical and public health settings. His research program has been grounded by a number of major project areas, including: 1) ethical implications of expanding newborn screening programs; 2) storage and use of perinatal and pediatric biological specimens for future research; 3) implications of genetics and gene-environment interactions for racial/ethnic minorities and other communities experiencing health disparities. In addition to these scholarly initiatives, Dr. Goldenberg is Director for Ethics, Policy and Practice for the National Newborn Screening Clearinghouse, also known as Baby’s First Test. He is a member of the Ethics and Legal Workgroup for the Newborn Screening Translational Research Network and the Legal and Legislative Workgroup for the American Public Health Laboratory Association. He is a member of the Pediatric Task Team for the Global Alliance for Genomics and Health.
JEAN CADIGANCo-Investigator
Jean Cadigan is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Social Medicine at the University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill. Dr. Cadigan received her PhD in anthropology from the University of California, Los Angeles. She then completed post-doctoral appointments in medical anthropology and human development. Her research interests include the ethical, legal, social, and policy implications of large-scale genetic research and translation. Dr. Cadigan has conducted interview projects with participants and investigators of genetic research studies about issues such as return of individual research results and ownership of biospecimens. She was heavily involved in the U.S. Biobank Study (Gail Henderson, PI) that surveyed biobanks about their policies and practices and used case studies to examine how policy-relevant decisions are made.

I have concerns about this study. With whom can I discuss this?

This study falls under the oversight of the Human Subjects Protection Program at the University of Louisville, which serves as a central institutional review board (IRB) for all sites involved in this study. The IRB is an independent committee made up of faculty and staff from the University of Louisville, as well as representatives of the Louisville community.

If you have any questions about your rights as a research subject, you may call the Human Subjects Protection Program Office at (502) 852-5188. You can discuss any questions about your rights as a research subject, in private, with a member of the IRB.

PUBLICATIONS

Modernizing Research Regulations Is Not Enough: It’s Time to Think Outside the Regulatory Box

  • American Journal of Bioethics  http://www.tandfonline.com.echo.louisville.edu/doi/full/10.1080/15265161.2017.1328899)

A call to the bioethics community and other stakeholders to move past the Common Rule and work approaches to human subjects protections that supersede regulations. In the ever-evolving world of biomedical research, regulations are not able to keep up. It is imperative that the bioethics community engage in a larger conversation about novel approaches best practices that go beyond regulation to address issues such as the identifiability of biological samples, the inclusion of underrepresented populations in research, and the development of practices to deal with international collaborations.

A Belmont Reboot: Building a Normative Foundation for Human Research in the 21st Century
